What is quantitative finance?

Traditional fund managers read news, talk to company executives and make calls based on experience and instinct. Quantitative managers do something fundamentally different: they write down every idea as a precise mathematical rule, then test it against decades of market data before risking a single dollar.

Think of it like cooking. A traditional chef improvises from taste. A quantitative chef writes every step down, measures everything precisely, and tests recipes in a lab until they produce the right result every time — then follows the recipe exactly, without exception.

The result is a disciplined, emotion-free process that can be measured, audited and improved. If a rule stops working, it changes. No ego, no attachment, no second-guessing at 2am.

"A quant's edge is systematic decision-making. By removing discretion and replacing it with rigorously tested models, they aim to profit from statistical patterns the human eye cannot reliably detect."

Under the hood

How quant analysis actually works

Before diving into specific strategies, it helps to understand the raw material quants work with and what they're looking for.

📡

Data is the raw material

Quants feed computers decades of price history, trading volume, interest rates, economic reports and even news sentiment. The more data, the more patterns can be found and tested — this is why the biggest quant funds invest heavily in data infrastructure.

🔍

Find a statistical edge

A "signal" is any measurable pattern that has historically predicted a price move — even slightly. For example: "when the VIX rises above 25 on a Tuesday, SPY tends to rise 1.2% over the next five days." Each signal is tested on historical data to see if the edge is real or just noise.

🤖

Automate the execution

Once a signal is validated, an algorithm watches for it in real-time and executes the trade automatically — without hesitation, without emotion, at any hour. This removes the biggest failure point in investing: the human mind under pressure.

💡
The key insight: quants don't need to predict the future. They just need an edge that's right slightly more than half the time — and then repeat that edge hundreds of times a year across many positions. Small, consistent wins compound into significant returns.
Profiting in both directions

Short selling — making money when prices fall

Most everyday investors can only profit when a stock rises. Buy low, sell high. But quants have access to a second direction: short selling. Understanding this unlocks how strategies like pairs trading work.

The short selling mechanic

Short selling sounds backwards — and it is. Instead of buying first and selling later, you sell first and buy later. Here's how it works in three steps:

1
Borrow & sell

You borrow 100 shares of Stock X at $50 from your broker and immediately sell them. You now hold $5,000 in cash — but you owe 100 shares back to the broker.

2
Wait for the fall

Stock X drops from $50 to $40. You buy back 100 shares for $4,000 — spending $1,000 less than you received when you sold.

3
Return & pocket the difference

You return the 100 shares to the broker. Profit = $1,000 (minus small borrowing fees). The whole trade works without ever "owning" the stock in the traditional sense.

Short selling at a glance

Borrow & sell at $50 × 100 = $5,000 in
Buy back at $40 × 100 = $4,000 out
Profit $1,000
⚠️
The key risk: if the stock rises instead of falls, losses are theoretically unlimited — a stock can go up forever. This is why quants pair short positions with long positions (like in pairs trading) to cap the risk.
Market-neutral strategy

Pairs trading — profit from the relationship, not the direction

Pairs trading combines a long position and a short position on two related stocks. Pioneered by quantitative researchers at Morgan Stanley in the 1980s, it's one of the most elegant market-neutral strategies ever designed.

The core idea

Find two stocks that historically move together — like Coca-Cola (KO) and PepsiCo (PEP). They're competitors in the same industry, so their prices tend to track each other over time. When one temporarily runs ahead of the other, you bet on the gap closing.

You go long the underperformer and short the overperformer — in equal dollar amounts. When the prices converge back to normal, both legs profit. The overall market direction is irrelevant.

🔑
Why "market neutral"? If the market crashes 20% during a pairs trade, both stocks would likely fall together — and the pairs trade still profits, because you only need the relative gap between them to close.

What triggers a trade

Three types of market signals we use

Every trade BPSQuant enters is triggered by at least one of these three well-documented phenomena. They've existed for decades because they're rooted in human psychology and market structure — not luck.

😨

Volatility (Fear) Premium

When markets get nervous, investors pay too much for protection — like paying $300/month for car insurance when your expected accident cost is only $100. We're the insurance company: we sell that overpriced protection systematically and collect the difference. This premium is reliable and persistent.

⏳

Time Decay (Theta)

Options lose value every single day they aren't needed — like a perishable product approaching its expiry date. An option expiring in 30 days loses value faster than one expiring in 90 days. We hold positions during that accelerating decay window and collect the daily erosion as income.

↩️

Mean Reversion

Markets overshoot. A stock that drops 15% in a week due to temporary bad news often snaps back. Algorithms detect when a move is statistically extreme — more than 2 standard deviations from normal — and position to profit from the return to average. No direction prediction needed.

From idea to live trade

Our five-step strategy development process

Every strategy starts as a hypothesis and doesn't touch real money until it survives the full gauntlet below. Most ideas fail — which is the point.

1

Hypothesis

We start with a precise, testable question: "Does IV typically overstate realised volatility by more than 3% in SPY?" or "Do 30-DTE puts on large-caps show a statistically higher win rate than 7-DTE puts?" If we can phrase it precisely, we can test it.

2

Backtest

The rule is run against at least 5 years of historical data, including at least one major crash. We study win rate, average gain vs. average loss, maximum drawdown, and how the strategy behaves in rising, flat, falling and crashing markets.

3

Stress test

We deliberately try to break the strategy. March 2020 crash? 2022 rate-hike selloff? Flat, low-volume summer of 2023? If the strategy can't survive these, it doesn't graduate — or we add rules to handle those regimes specifically.

4

Paper trade

Before real money, the strategy runs in live-market simulation for at least 60 days. This catches what backtests miss: real bid-ask spreads, execution delays, slippage, and broker quirks. Many strategies die here — and that's exactly the point.

5

Live — with guardrails

Live trades start at minimum size. Position limits, drawdown circuit breakers, and volatility scaling are active from day one. We scale up only after the live track record matches backtest expectations. Any drift triggers an investigation before adding more capital.

Portfolio construction

Building a portfolio — not just picking trades

A single good trade isn't enough. A quant's job is to combine many strategies and positions so that the overall portfolio maximises risk-adjusted return — meaning the best return for the least amount of risk taken.

🎯

Optimised weights

Given a universe of assets and a set of constraints (max position size, max sector exposure, correlation limits), algorithms find the combination of position weights that historically delivered the highest Sharpe ratio — the industry's standard measure of risk-adjusted return.

🔄

Diversification by design

Quant portfolios often run dozens of uncorrelated strategies simultaneously. If one stops working — a signal breaks down, a pair permanently decouples — the impact on the portfolio is small. No single strategy makes or breaks the fund.

📏

Dynamic rebalancing

Weights aren't set once and forgotten. As market conditions change — volatility rises, correlations shift, new signals emerge — the portfolio is rebalanced automatically. The system adapts continuously without human intervention or emotional bias.

Risk management

Why protecting the downside matters more than chasing the upside

A fund that loses 50% needs to earn 100% just to break even. Mathematically, avoiding large losses is more powerful than maximising gains. Every BPSQuant system is designed around this truth.

BPSQuant in practice

How we apply all of this

BPSQuant focuses on one specific, well-documented edge: the volatility risk premium in large-cap US ETFs — combined with systematic mean-reversion and pairs strategies. Liquid, measurable, and consistently income-generating.

📊

Daily data screening

Every morning, algorithms scan implied vs. realised volatility across SPY, QQQ and DIA, check pair spreads for divergence signals, and flag any mean-reversion setups. We only enter when the edge is present — if it isn't, we wait.

⚖️

Volatility-based sizing

Position size is recalculated daily using current market volatility. In calm periods, we can deploy more capital. When volatility spikes, positions automatically shrink — so a turbulent week doesn't become a fund-level event.

🛡️

Defined-risk structures

Every trade — including pair trades and short positions — enters with a known maximum loss. Spreads, hedges and stop-loss rules ensure there are no "tail risk" surprises. We know the worst case before we enter, and price the trade to make it worth it.
