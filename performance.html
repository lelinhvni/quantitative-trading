Quantitative Finance

How math and data become returns

Quantitative investing sounds intimidating, but the core idea is simple: use rules and data instead of gut feeling. This page walks you through exactly how it works — no finance degree needed.

What is quantitative finance?

Traditional fund managers read news, talk to company executives and make calls based on experience. Quantitative managers do something different: they write down their ideas as mathematical rules and test them against decades of market data before risking a single dollar.

Think of it like cooking. A traditional chef improvises from taste and instinct. A quantitative chef writes every step down, measures everything precisely, and tests recipes in a lab until they consistently produce the right result. Then they follow the recipe exactly — every time.

The result is a disciplined, emotion-free process that can be measured, audited and improved. If something stops working, the rules change. No ego, no attachment.

The toolkit

Three types of market signals we use

Every trade we enter is triggered by at least one of these three well-documented phenomena. They've existed for decades because they're rooted in human psychology and market structure — not luck.

😨

Volatility (Fear) Premium

When markets get nervous, investors pay too much for protection — like paying $300/month for car insurance when your expected accident cost is $100/month. We're the insurance company, selling that overpriced protection systematically. This premium is reliable and persistent.

⏳

Time Decay (Theta)

Options lose value every single day they aren't needed. An option that expires in 30 days loses value faster than one expiring in 90 days — like how a perishable product drops in price as it approaches its expiry date. We hold positions during that accelerating decay window.

↩️

Mean Reversion

Markets overshoot. A stock that drops 15% in a week because of temporary bad news often snaps back. Algorithms detect when moves are statistically extreme and position to profit from the return to normal — without needing to predict when the reversal happens.

The process

From idea to live trade: our five-step process

Every strategy starts as a hypothesis and doesn't touch real money until it survives the full gauntlet below.

1

Hypothesis

We start with a simple question: "Is there a repeatable edge here?" Examples: "Does IV typically overstate realised volatility by more than 3% in SPY?" or "Do 30-DTE puts on large caps have a statistically higher win rate than 7-DTE puts?" If we can phrase it precisely, we can test it.

2

Backtest

We run the rule against historical data going back at least 5 years, including a market crash. We look at win rate, average gain vs. average loss, drawdown, and how the strategy behaves in different market regimes (rising, flat, falling, crashing).

3

Stress test

We deliberately try to break the strategy. What happens in the March 2020 crash? In the 2022 rate-hike selloff? In a flat, low-volume market? If the strategy can't survive these, it doesn't graduate — or we add rules to handle those cases specifically.

4

Paper trade

Before real money, the strategy runs in simulation for at least 60 days in live market conditions. This catches implementation details that backtests miss: bid-ask spreads, execution delays, slippage, broker-specific quirks. Many strategies die here — and that's the point.

5

Live — with guardrails

Live trades start at minimum size. Position limits, drawdown circuit breakers, and volatility scaling are all active from day one. We scale up only after the live track record matches our backtest expectations. If it drifts, we investigate before adding more capital.

Risk management

Why managing the downside matters more than chasing the upside

A fund that loses 50% needs to earn 100% just to break even. Protecting capital during bad periods is mathematically more important than maximising gains during good ones. Our systems are designed around this truth.

JSS in practice

How JSS applies quant methods to income generation

We focus on one specific edge: the volatility risk premium in large-cap US ETFs. It's well-documented, liquid, and generates consistent income — not dramatic highs, but reliable, compounding returns.

📊

Daily data screening

Every morning, algorithms scan implied vs. realised volatility across SPY, QQQ and DIA. We only enter when the premium is wide enough — if the edge isn't there, we wait. No trades for the sake of it.

⚖️

Volatility-based sizing

Position size is calculated daily based on current market volatility. In calm markets, we can deploy more capital. When volatility spikes, we automatically shrink — so a volatile period doesn't become a disaster.

🛡️

Defined-risk structures

We use spreads and hedged positions so every single trade has a known maximum loss before we enter. There are no "tail risk" surprises — we know the worst case and price the return to make it worth it.
